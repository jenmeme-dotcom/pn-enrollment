# ACLS Live Course Instructor Guide

Version: 2026-07-10

Purpose: This is an original, AHA-aligned facilitation guide for teaching a live Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support course. It is designed to help an instructor organize lessons, run live practice, and speak clearly during class.

Important certification note: This document is not an official American Heart Association Instructor Manual, Provider Manual, exam, skills testing sheet, or course video. To issue an AHA ACLS Provider completion card, the course must be delivered through an authorized AHA Training Center/Training Site by an authorized AHA Instructor, using the current required AHA course materials, videos, exams, skills testing, eCards, and Training Center policies.

Instructor safety note: Verify all algorithms, medication doses, device energy settings, and local protocols against the current AHA ACLS Provider Manual, Instructor Manual, local medical direction, and facility policy before class.

## Course Positioning

Audience:
- Physicians, nurses, paramedics, respiratory therapists, advanced practice providers, and other healthcare professionals who manage adult cardiopulmonary emergencies.

Learner expectations:
- Students should arrive proficient in BLS skills.
- Students should complete required AHA prework/precourse requirements before class when applicable.
- Students should be ready to interpret rhythms, operate a defibrillator, deliver high-quality CPR, use team communication, and participate in megacode scenarios.

Course goal:
- Students will recognize and manage adult respiratory arrest, cardiac arrest, bradycardia, tachycardia, post-cardiac arrest care, and selected peri-arrest emergencies using high-quality CPR, early defibrillation, effective team roles, and current ACLS algorithms.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. Demonstrate high-quality adult BLS integrated into ACLS.
2. Use a systematic assessment approach: initial impression, BLS assessment, primary assessment, secondary assessment, and differential diagnosis.
3. Identify and manage respiratory arrest and inadequate ventilation.
4. Recognize shockable and nonshockable cardiac arrest rhythms.
5. Lead or participate in adult cardiac arrest management using ACLS algorithms.
6. Recognize and manage symptomatic bradycardia.
7. Recognize and manage stable and unstable tachyarrhythmias.
8. Apply team dynamics: closed-loop communication, role assignment, mutual support, and concise debriefing.
9. Identify reversible causes of arrest and peri-arrest deterioration.
10. Describe immediate post-ROSC priorities.

## Equipment and Room Setup

Minimum equipment:
- Adult manikin with compression feedback if available
- Bag-mask device, oxygen setup, suction
- AED/defibrillator trainer with pads
- Monitor rhythm simulator
- Airway adjuncts and advanced airway demonstration equipment
- IV/IO training supplies
- Mock code medications or labels
- Timer or metronome
- Whiteboard or projected algorithm cards
- Current official AHA student and instructor materials

Room layout:
- Put the manikin in the center with enough room for compressor rotation.
- Place defibrillator/monitor on the patient's left or at the team leader's line of sight.
- Assign zones: airway, compressor, monitor/defibrillator, medication/IV, recorder, team leader.
- Keep pathways clear. Students should be able to rotate quickly without moving furniture.

## Certification-Safe Class Opening Script

Read aloud:

"Welcome to ACLS. Today is about organized, high-quality resuscitation. ACLS is not a collection of memorized drug doses. It is a system: recognize the problem, start excellent BLS, call for help, attach the monitor, use the correct algorithm, communicate clearly, and reassess every two minutes.

For AHA certification, we will follow the official AHA course requirements, materials, videos, skills testing, and exam process used by our Training Center. This guide supports how I facilitate the class, but the official AHA materials are the authority for certification.

In every case today, I want you to think out loud. Say what rhythm you see, whether the patient has a pulse, whether the patient is stable or unstable, what intervention comes next, and who is doing each task. If you are unsure, use your team. ACLS is a team sport."

## Teaching Method

Use a repeated pattern:

1. Tell students the clinical decision.
2. Show the action.
3. Let them practice immediately.
4. Pause after the scenario for a short debrief:
   - What went well?
   - What was the first priority?
   - What did the rhythm tell us?
   - What delayed care?
   - What will we do differently next time?

## Core Instructor Language

Use these phrases repeatedly:

- "First, check responsiveness and breathing."
- "Call for help and activate the emergency response system."
- "Start high-quality CPR."
- "Attach the monitor/defibrillator."
- "Is the rhythm shockable?"
- "Do we have a pulse?"
- "What is our next action in the algorithm?"
- "Resume CPR immediately after the shock."
- "Switch compressors at the two-minute mark."
- "Avoid excessive ventilation."
- "Treat reversible causes."
- "Use closed-loop communication."
- "Team leader, summarize the plan."

## Module 1: High-Quality CPR and BLS Integration

Key teaching points:
- ACLS depends on excellent BLS.
- Start compressions quickly.
- Push hard and fast, allow full recoil, minimize pauses, avoid excessive ventilation.
- Rotate compressors every 2 minutes or sooner if fatigued.
- If no advanced airway is in place, use 30 compressions to 2 breaths.
- If an advanced airway is in place, continue compressions and give 1 breath every 6 seconds.

Read aloud:

"Before we talk about medications, airways, or advanced rhythms, we have to get the foundation right. The intervention most likely to be happening continuously during a cardiac arrest is CPR. If CPR quality drops, everything else drops with it. Good ACLS means the team protects compressions. We pause only when we need to check rhythm, shock, or perform a critical action, and then we get right back on the chest."

Live demonstration:
- Show hand position, compression depth, rate, recoil, and switching compressors.
- Demonstrate a two-minute CPR cycle with a timer.
- Show how ventilation can become excessive and how to correct it.

Practice prompt:
"You are first to the bedside. The patient is unresponsive and not breathing normally. Show me the first 30 seconds of care."

Expected actions:
- Check responsiveness.
- Call for help.
- Check breathing and pulse quickly.
- Start compressions.
- Ask for monitor/defibrillator and oxygen.

Debrief:
"The first 30 seconds should look simple and decisive. Recognition, help, compressions, defibrillator. That sequence saves time."

## Module 2: Team Dynamics

Key teaching points:
- The team leader keeps the big picture.
- Team members perform assigned tasks and confirm orders.
- Closed-loop communication prevents missed actions.
- The recorder tracks times, rhythm checks, shocks, medications, airway status, and ROSC signs.

Read aloud:

"A disorganized code is usually not a knowledge problem; it is a communication problem. The team leader should not be doing every task. The leader assigns roles, listens to updates, and makes the next decision. When I give an order, I want the team member to repeat it back, complete it, and report completion."

Closed-loop example:
- Leader: "Chris, give epinephrine 1 milligram IV now."
- Chris: "Epinephrine 1 milligram IV now."
- Chris: "Epinephrine 1 milligram IV is in."
- Leader: "Confirmed. Recorder, mark epinephrine at 10:14."

Practice:
- Assign students to leader, compressor, airway, monitor, medication, recorder.
- Run a 60-second communication drill with no clinical complexity.

## Module 3: Systematic Approach and Respiratory Arrest

Key teaching points:
- Use assessment before jumping to treatment.
- Determine if the patient is breathing adequately and has a pulse.
- Respiratory arrest with a pulse requires ventilation and reassessment.
- Avoid hyperventilation.
- Consider naloxone when opioid-associated emergency is suspected and local protocols support it.

Read aloud:

"The systematic approach keeps us from skipping critical information. First impression: does the patient look sick? BLS assessment: are they responsive, breathing, and pulseless? Primary assessment: airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure. Secondary assessment: history, medications, events, and reversible causes. In respiratory arrest, the patient may still have a pulse. That means we ventilate, support oxygenation, monitor closely, and prepare because they can deteriorate into cardiac arrest."

Scenario prompt:
"A 58-year-old patient is found unresponsive after receiving pain medication. They are not breathing adequately. Pulse is present at 54. Oxygen saturation is falling."

Expected actions:
- Call for help.
- Open airway.
- Provide bag-mask ventilation with oxygen.
- Attach monitor and pulse oximeter.
- Reassess pulse and breathing.
- Consider opioid reversal per protocol.
- Prepare for advanced airway if needed.

Debrief:
"The key decision was pulse present versus pulseless. With a pulse, we ventilate and monitor. If the pulse disappears, we transition immediately to cardiac arrest care."

## Module 4: Adult Cardiac Arrest Algorithm

Key teaching points:
- Start CPR, ventilate, give oxygen, attach monitor/defibrillator.
- Decide shockable versus nonshockable.
- Shockable: VF or pulseless VT.
- Nonshockable: asystole or PEA.
- CPR cycles are organized around 2-minute intervals.
- Defibrillate shockable rhythms quickly.
- Give epinephrine during arrest per current algorithm.
- For refractory VF/pVT, consider amiodarone or lidocaine.
- Search for reversible causes.

Read aloud:

"Every adult cardiac arrest begins the same way: start CPR, ventilate with oxygen, and attach the monitor/defibrillator. Then the rhythm determines the path. Shockable rhythms need defibrillation. Nonshockable rhythms need CPR, epinephrine, and aggressive search for reversible causes. After every shock, we resume CPR immediately. We do not stand around admiring the monitor."

Rhythm decision script:

"Team, rhythm check. Pause compressions. I see ventricular fibrillation. Resume compressions while the defibrillator charges. This is shockable. Everyone clear? Shock delivered. Resume CPR immediately. Start the next two-minute cycle."

PEA/asystole decision script:

"Team, rhythm check. Pause compressions. I see organized electrical activity. Pulse check: no pulse. This is PEA. Resume CPR. Give epinephrine now and start searching for reversible causes."

Reversible causes memory list:
- Hypovolemia
- Hypoxia
- Hydrogen ion/acidosis
- Hypo-/hyperkalemia
- Hypothermia
- Tension pneumothorax
- Cardiac tamponade
- Toxins
- Pulmonary thrombosis
- Coronary thrombosis

Instructor caution:
- Teach reversible causes as clinical thinking, not as a chant. Ask students what evidence supports each cause.

## Module 5: VF/pVT Arrest Station

Case stem:
"A 62-year-old patient collapses in the emergency department. They are unresponsive, gasping, and pulseless. Monitor shows coarse ventricular fibrillation."

Expected sequence:
1. Start CPR and ventilations.
2. Attach monitor/defibrillator pads.
3. Identify VF/pVT as shockable.
4. Defibrillate using device-specific energy settings.
5. Resume CPR immediately for 2 minutes.
6. Establish IV/IO access.
7. Continue rhythm checks every 2 minutes.
8. Give epinephrine per algorithm timing.
9. Consider advanced airway and waveform capnography.
10. For persistent VF/pVT, give amiodarone or lidocaine per current algorithm.
11. Treat reversible causes.
12. Transition to post-cardiac arrest care if ROSC occurs.

Read aloud during scenario:

"You have a pulseless patient. Start CPR. Assign roles. Pads are on. At the rhythm check, tell me if it is shockable or nonshockable. If it is shockable, charge while compressions continue, clear safely, deliver the shock, and immediately resume CPR. Do not delay compressions for medication preparation."

Debrief:
"What protected CPR quality? What caused pauses? Did the team leader announce the rhythm and next action clearly? Was defibrillation delivered as soon as possible?"

## Module 6: PEA/Asystole Arrest Station

Case stem:
"A 74-year-old patient becomes unresponsive on the medical floor. They are pulseless. Monitor shows a narrow complex rhythm at 38."

Expected sequence:
1. Start CPR.
2. Attach monitor/defibrillator.
3. Identify nonshockable rhythm.
4. Give epinephrine as soon as feasible.
5. Continue CPR in 2-minute cycles.
6. Consider advanced airway and capnography.
7. Search for reversible causes using clinical clues.
8. Recheck rhythm and pulse every 2 minutes.
9. If rhythm becomes shockable, move to shockable pathway.
10. If ROSC occurs, move to post-cardiac arrest care.

Read aloud:

"PEA is not a rhythm we shock. It is a clinical state: organized electrical activity without a pulse. The treatment is CPR, epinephrine, and fixing the cause. The monitor is not enough. Look at the patient, the history, the oxygenation, the volume status, the potassium, the signs of tamponade or tension pneumothorax, and the possibility of thrombosis or toxins."

Debrief:
"The mistake in PEA is staring at the monitor and waiting for it to change. The work is CPR plus cause-finding."

## Module 7: Bradycardia With a Pulse

Key teaching points:
- Determine if the patient has a pulse.
- Assess for symptoms and cardiopulmonary compromise.
- Support airway, breathing, oxygenation, monitoring, IV access, and 12-lead ECG.
- For symptomatic bradycardia, atropine is commonly first medication unless contraindicated or unlikely to work.
- If atropine is ineffective, consider transcutaneous pacing and/or dopamine or epinephrine infusion.
- Seek expert consultation and consider transvenous pacing when needed.

Current public AHA algorithm dosing reminders to verify against official materials:
- Atropine: 1 mg IV bolus, repeat every 3 to 5 minutes, maximum total 3 mg.
- Dopamine infusion: usual 5 to 20 mcg/kg/min, titrate to response.
- Epinephrine infusion: 2 to 10 mcg/min, titrate to response.

Read aloud:

"Bradycardia with a pulse is not treated by the heart rate alone. We treat the patient. Ask: is there hypotension, acutely altered mental status, signs of shock, ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure? If the patient is unstable because of the bradycardia, we act. If they are stable, we monitor, identify causes, and prepare."

Scenario prompt:
"A 69-year-old patient is pale, diaphoretic, confused, and hypotensive. Heart rate is 32 with a pulse."

Expected actions:
- Identify symptomatic bradycardia.
- Support ABCs, oxygen if needed, monitor, IV access, 12-lead ECG.
- Give atropine if appropriate.
- Prepare pacing.
- Start dopamine or epinephrine infusion if needed.
- Call expert help.

Debrief:
"The critical thinking point is whether the bradycardia is causing poor perfusion. If yes, treatment should not wait."

## Module 8: Tachyarrhythmia With a Pulse

Key teaching points:
- Confirm pulse.
- Determine stability.
- Unstable tachycardia generally requires synchronized cardioversion.
- Stable tachycardia requires rhythm analysis: narrow or wide QRS, regular or irregular.
- Consider vagal maneuvers and adenosine for appropriate regular narrow-complex tachycardias.
- Wide-complex tachycardia should be treated carefully and often with expert consultation.

Current public AHA algorithm dosing reminders to verify against official materials:
- Adenosine: 6 mg rapid IV push followed by saline flush; second dose 12 mg if needed.
- Synchronized cardioversion: follow device recommendations and current guideline sections; if unknown, use maximum energy setting per 2025 algorithm language.
- Stable wide-QRS tachycardia antiarrhythmic options may include procainamide, amiodarone, or sotalol depending on rhythm, QT, heart failure status, contraindications, and protocol.

Read aloud:

"For tachycardia with a pulse, the first fork in the road is stability. If the patient is unstable because of the tachycardia, prepare for synchronized cardioversion. If the patient is stable, slow down enough to classify the rhythm: narrow or wide, regular or irregular. That classification guides medications and consultation."

Scenario prompt, unstable:
"A 55-year-old patient has a heart rate of 190, narrow regular rhythm, hypotension, chest pressure, and altered mental status."

Expected actions:
- Identify unstable tachycardia.
- Prepare synchronized cardioversion.
- Sedate if feasible, but do not delay urgent cardioversion.
- Cardiovert and reassess.

Scenario prompt, stable:
"A 42-year-old patient is awake with palpitations. Blood pressure is stable. Monitor shows regular narrow-complex tachycardia at 180."

Expected actions:
- Monitor and IV access.
- 12-lead ECG if available.
- Consider vagal maneuvers.
- Consider adenosine if appropriate.
- Reassess rhythm and patient condition.

Debrief:
"The key phrase is 'unstable because of the tachycardia.' Do not cardiovert every fast heart rate, and do not delay cardioversion when the tachycardia is causing shock."

## Module 9: Acute Coronary Syndromes and Stroke Systems

Key teaching points:
- ACLS includes recognition and routing, not definitive hospital management of every condition.
- Chest pain and suspected ACS require rapid assessment, 12-lead ECG, aspirin when appropriate, oxygen only when indicated, and early reperfusion pathway activation.
- Stroke care requires last-known-well time, neurologic screening, glucose check, rapid transport/imaging, and stroke team activation.

Read aloud:

"ACLS is also about preventing arrest. ACS and stroke are system problems. The individual clinician recognizes the pattern, starts appropriate initial care, gets the ECG or stroke screen, and activates the pathway. Time matters, but so does choosing the right destination and team."

Practice questions:
- "What time did symptoms start?"
- "When was the patient last known well?"
- "What does the 12-lead ECG show?"
- "Does this patient need a STEMI alert, stroke alert, or both?"

## Module 10: Post-Cardiac Arrest Care

Key teaching points:
- ROSC is not the finish line.
- Optimize oxygenation and ventilation.
- Treat hypotension and shock.
- Obtain 12-lead ECG and identify the cause.
- Consider coronary reperfusion pathways when indicated.
- Manage temperature according to current guidelines and local protocol.
- Monitor for seizures and neurologic recovery.
- Transfer to appropriate critical care.

Read aloud:

"When the pulse comes back, the team changes gears. We stop doing cardiac arrest care and start preventing rearrest and brain injury. Check blood pressure, oxygenation, ventilation, glucose, temperature strategy, 12-lead ECG, and the likely cause. The patient needs organized critical care, not a celebration and a hallway handoff."

Post-ROSC handoff script:

"This is a 62-year-old patient with witnessed VF arrest. CPR started immediately. They received multiple shocks and epinephrine per algorithm. Amiodarone or lidocaine was given for refractory VF/pVT per protocol. ROSC occurred at 10:26. Current rhythm is sinus tachycardia, blood pressure is 92/58, oxygen saturation is 96% with assisted ventilation. We need post-arrest stabilization, 12-lead ECG, labs, temperature management per protocol, and ICU/cath lab decision-making."

## Megacode Case 1: Shockable Arrest

Setup:
- Patient: 60-year-old, collapses during triage.
- Initial rhythm: VF.
- Pulse: absent.
- Progression: VF persists through first cycles, then ROSC after appropriate care.

Instructor prompts:
- "The patient is unresponsive and not breathing normally."
- "Monitor shows VF."
- "After the shock, what should happen immediately?"
- "Two minutes have passed. Rhythm check."
- "ETCO2 rises abruptly and pulse is present."

Expected leadership behaviors:
- Assigns roles.
- Starts CPR immediately.
- Identifies shockable rhythm.
- Defibrillates quickly and safely.
- Resumes CPR immediately.
- Tracks 2-minute cycles and medications.
- Uses closed-loop communication.
- Moves to post-ROSC care.

Debrief questions:
- "How long were compression pauses?"
- "Did anyone verbalize shockable versus nonshockable?"
- "Was the team ready for post-ROSC care?"

## Megacode Case 2: Nonshockable Arrest From Hypoxia

Setup:
- Patient: 48-year-old with severe asthma or respiratory failure.
- Initial rhythm: PEA.
- Pulse: absent.
- Clue: difficult ventilation, wheezing or poor air movement.
- Progression: improves after ventilation/oxygenation and appropriate care.

Instructor prompts:
- "The patient is pulseless."
- "Monitor shows organized narrow rhythm."
- "Bag-mask ventilation is difficult."
- "Oxygen saturation is low."
- "After airway optimization and CPR, the patient develops a pulse."

Expected actions:
- CPR.
- No shock for PEA.
- Epinephrine per algorithm.
- Airway and ventilation management.
- Treat likely hypoxia/bronchospasm per protocol.
- Reassess rhythm and pulse.

Debrief:
"This case was about identifying the cause. PEA is often fixed by fixing the physiology."

## Megacode Case 3: Symptomatic Bradycardia Deteriorating to Arrest

Setup:
- Patient: 70-year-old with inferior MI symptoms.
- Initial rhythm: bradycardia with pulse, hypotension.
- Progression: if delayed care, pulseless arrest; if appropriate care, improves.

Expected actions:
- Assess stability.
- Support ABCs.
- IV access, monitor, 12-lead ECG.
- Atropine if appropriate.
- Prepare pacing.
- Consider epinephrine or dopamine infusion.
- Expert consultation.

Debrief:
"Bradycardia cases test whether we treat poor perfusion early enough."

## Megacode Case 4: Unstable Tachycardia

Setup:
- Patient: 55-year-old with HR 190, hypotension, chest discomfort, altered mentation.
- Rhythm: regular narrow-complex tachycardia or monomorphic wide-complex tachycardia.

Expected actions:
- Confirm pulse.
- Identify instability.
- Prepare synchronized cardioversion.
- Sedate if feasible without delaying therapy.
- Reassess after cardioversion.
- Obtain expert consultation and treat cause.

Debrief:
"The major decision was not the exact rhythm name. The major decision was unstable tachycardia requiring electricity."

## Student-Friendly Summary Script

Read aloud near the end:

"If you remember one framework, remember this: in arrest, start CPR and attach the defibrillator. Then ask shockable or nonshockable. Shockable rhythms need defibrillation and immediate CPR afterward. Nonshockable rhythms need CPR, epinephrine, and a search for causes. In bradycardia and tachycardia with a pulse, treat the patient, not just the number. Stable patients give you time to diagnose. Unstable patients need immediate intervention. In every case, communicate clearly, close the loop, and reassess."

## Quick Reference: What to Say When Students Freeze

- "What is the pulse status?"
- "Is the patient stable or unstable?"
- "Is the rhythm shockable?"
- "What does the algorithm say comes next?"
- "Who is doing compressions?"
- "Who is managing airway?"
- "Who has the defibrillator?"
- "Who is recording time and medications?"
- "What reversible cause fits this patient?"
- "What changed after our intervention?"

## Closing Script

"You do not need to be the loudest person in the room to be a strong ACLS provider. You need to be organized, clear, and committed to the basics. Start CPR. Defibrillate shockable rhythms. Ventilate appropriately. Give medications at the right time. Look for reversible causes. Communicate with closed loop. Reassess. That is how a group of clinicians becomes a resuscitation team."
